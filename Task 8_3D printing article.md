# Introduction to 3D Printing

## What is 3D Printing?

3D printing is a process of creating a physical object from a digital design.
It works by adding material layer by layer until the full object is formed.
This is why it is also called **Additive Manufacturing**.

Think of it like building a wall brick by brick — except the printer does it
automatically, one thin layer at a time.

---

## How Does a 3D Printer Work?

1. You start with a **3D model** — a digital file of the object you want to print.
2. The model is saved as an **STL file** (Standard Tessellation Language).
3. The STL file is loaded into a **slicer software** like Ultimaker Cura or
   Creality Slicer.
4. The slicer cuts the model into hundreds of thin horizontal layers and
   generates a **G-code** file.
5. The G-code is sent to the printer, which follows the instructions to print
   each layer one by one.

---

## Types of 3D Printing

There are three main types of 3D printing technologies:

### 1. FDM — Fused Deposition Modeling
- Most common and affordable type
- Melts a plastic filament (usually **PLA or ABS**) and deposits it layer by layer
- Used in most desktop 3D printers

### 2. SLA — Stereolithography
- Uses a **UV laser** to cure liquid resin into solid layers
- Produces very smooth and detailed prints
- More expensive than FDM

### 3. SLS — Selective Laser Sintering
- Uses a **laser** to fuse powdered material (nylon, metal) together
- Used in industrial manufacturing
- Very accurate and strong parts

## Key Printer Settings to Know

| Setting | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Bed Temperature** | The temperature of the print surface. PLA needs around 60°C |
| **Nozzle Temperature** | Temperature at which filament melts. PLA melts at 190–210°C |
| **Infill Density** | How solid the inside of the print is. 20% is common for normal use |
| **Layer Height** | Thickness of each layer. Lower = smoother but slower |
| **Print Speed** | How fast the nozzle moves. Slower = better quality |

---

## What is PLA?

PLA (Polylactic Acid) is the most commonly used filament material for beginners.

- It is made from **cornstarch or sugarcane** — biodegradable!
- Easy to print with, does not warp much
- Available in many colors
- Not suitable for high heat environments

---

## Applications of 3D Printing

3D printing is used in many fields today:

- **Medicine** — printing prosthetics, dental implants, and even organs
- **Aerospace** — lightweight parts for rockets and satellites
- **Education** — printing models for learning
- **Architecture** — scale models of buildings
- **Consumer products** — phone cases, toys, spare parts

---

## Conclusion

3D printing has changed the way we design and manufacture objects. What once
took days in a factory can now be done in hours on a desktop printer. As the
technology becomes cheaper and more accessible, it is becoming an essential
skill for engineers, designers, and makers worldwide.
